# ROUND 5 FINDINGS: SUPERPOSITION AND WAVE MECHANICS

## Executive Summary

The superposition equation (ESQUISSE Section IX) rests on two central claims: that human locomotion contains 7 independent oscillatory modes whose incommensurate frequency ratios produce a fractal trajectory of dimension d ~ 1.5, and that the kinetic chain in rotational athletics sequences segment activations at golden ratio timing intervals. Both claims are refuted by the biomechanical evidence. Only 3 truly independent oscillatory generators exist during walking (locomotor CPG, cardiac pacemaker, respiratory center), with the other 4 claimed "modes" being mechanically coupled manifestations of the gait oscillator. Furthermore, biological oscillators actively entrain to integer frequency ratios -- the opposite of the incommensurability the framework requires. The golden ratio kinetic chain hypothesis fails across all three sports examined: baseball pitching ratios are 0.66 and 2.57 (not phi), golf shows a U-shaped delay pattern incompatible with a Fibonacci cascade, and the tennis serve violates proximal-to-distal sequencing entirely. The fractal dimension of healthy gait variability is D ~ 1.13-1.25 (from DFA alpha ~ 0.76-0.87), not D = 1.5 -- confirming and strengthening Round 4's finding that D = 1.5 corresponds to the pathological state.

## The 7-Mode Question

### Which modes are truly independent vs mechanically coupled?

The ESQUISSE (DEF-08) claims seven concurrent oscillatory modes during human movement: postural sway, respiratory rhythm, gait cadence, cardiac rhythm, arm swing, vertical oscillation, and ground reaction force transient. Systematic verification reveals that only 3 of these are generated by independent oscillatory mechanisms. The other 4 are either mechanically coupled responses to the gait oscillator or categorically not oscillatory modes at all.

**Independent oscillatory generators (3):**

| Mode | Generator | Frequency during walking | Independence basis |
|------|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| Locomotor CPG | Spinal central pattern generator | 1.6-2.1 Hz (step frequency) | Primary rhythm; all mechanical oscillations derive from it |
| Cardiac pacemaker | Sinoatrial node (autonomous) | 1.5-2.0 Hz (100-120 BPM) | Intrinsic electrical oscillator; can free-run independently |
| Respiratory center | Brainstem respiratory nuclei | 0.33-0.42 Hz (20-25 breaths/min) | Independent neural oscillator; can be voluntarily overridden |

**Coupled manifestations of the gait oscillator (3):**

| Claimed mode | Actual status | Evidence |
|--------------|--------------|----------|
| Arm swing (1.5-3.0 Hz) | Driven by gait, not independent | Frequency identical to step frequency by definition. Collins et al. 2009 (PMC2817299): passive walking model with free-swinging arms produces human-like arm swing with zero active torques. Active neural component augments but does not create independent oscillation |
| Vertical COM oscillation (1.5-3.0 Hz) | Geometric consequence of gait | Inverted pendulum walking model: COM rises at mid-stance, falls at double support. Frequency = step frequency. Phase relationship is deterministic, not a "shift" from gait. Amplitude ~4-5 cm peak-to-peak. Listing this separately is like counting x and y components of a pendulum as independent oscillators |
| Pelvic/trunk rotation | Coupled oscillator dependent on gait speed | Pelvis and thorax counter-rotate at stride frequency. At low speeds: near in-phase. At high speeds: out-of-phase. The phase relationship is speed-dependent, confirming coupling to the primary gait oscillator |

**Categorically not an oscillatory mode (1):**

| Claimed mode | Actual status | Evidence |
|--------------|--------------|----------|
| GRF heel strike transient (10-30 Hz) | Broadband impulse event, not an oscillation | Duration: 10-20 ms. Occurs once per step at ~10% of stance phase. Frequency content extends to 400 Hz -- precisely BECAUSE it is not periodic. Rearfoot strike spectral peak: 27 Hz; non-rearfoot: 15 Hz. An oscillatory mode requires continuous periodic behavior. A transient impact is to an oscillation as a gunshot is to a foghorn. This is a category error |

**Not listed but present (all coupled):**

Head stabilization (vertical at 2 Hz, lateral at 1 Hz -- driven by trunk oscillation), mediolateral COM sway (at stride frequency), and additional trunk-pelvis counter-rotation patterns all exist as observable oscillations during walking, but all are mechanically coupled to the primary gait oscillator. They further demonstrate that the number of observable oscillating body segments far exceeds the number of independent oscillatory generators.

The distinction matters because the superposition equation's fractal dimension prediction depends on independent, incommensurate frequencies. Mechanically coupled oscillations share the same frequency generator and therefore cannot contribute independent frequency components to the superposition.

**Key sources:** Collins et al. 2009 (PMC2817299) on passive arm swing dynamics; Wagenaar & van Emmerik 2000 on biomechanical transitions in arm/leg swing; Kuhtz-Buschbeck & Jing 2012 on arm swing drive; Taga 1995 on nonlinear dynamics of locomotor CPG; Ivanenko et al. on muscle synergies during walking (PMC9377677); Pozzo et al. on head frequency during locomotion.

### What are the actual frequency ranges for healthy walking?

Round 1 noted that the ESQUISSE frequency ranges were "too broad." Round 5 provides the corrected values:

| Mode | ESQUISSE claim | Actual during normal walking | Issue |
|------|---------------|------------------------------|-------|

### Entrainment: biological oscillators lock to integer ratios

The superposition equation (ESQUISSE Problem 8.2) assumes that the frequency ratios f_i/f_j are incommensurate, producing a quasi-periodic trajectory. The biological evidence shows the opposite: the three independent oscillators actively seek integer frequency ratios through neural and mechanical coupling.

**Locomotor-respiratory coupling:**

The dominant observed ratio during walking is 2:1 (two steps per breath). Also observed: 4:1, 3:2, 5:2. Approximately 50% of subjects show clear entrainment during walking (Bernasconi & Kohl 1993). The respiratory oscillator ADJUSTS its frequency to lock with the gait oscillator. At 2:1 coupling with gait at 1.9 Hz step frequency, respiratory rate adjusts to ~0.95 Hz (stride frequency), well within the physiological range during exercise.

**Cardiolocomotor coupling:**

Heart rate spontaneously locks to step frequency at simple integer ratios (1:1, 2:1, 3:2). Entrainment is observed predominantly at faster speeds, with entrainment bands matching step frequency. This coupling improves cardiovascular efficiency -- better stroke volume, lower oxygen consumption (PMC7890119).

**The Farey tree framework:**

The coupling dynamics follow the Farey tree hierarchy of rational frequency ratios, with simpler ratios (2:1, 3:1) being more robust attractors than complex ones. The mathematical concept of incommensurability assumes fixed, non-interacting frequencies. Biological oscillators are neither fixed nor non-interacting. They are coupled nonlinear oscillators that seek phase-locked integer ratios.

This directly contradicts the ESQUISSE's reliance on incommensurate frequencies to generate quasi-periodicity. Biological oscillators produce the OPPOSITE of incommensurability -- they produce commensurability through active entrainment.

**Key sources:** Bernasconi & Kohl 1993 on locomotor-respiratory coupling; O'Halloran et al. 2012; Bramble & Carrier 1983 on coupling in humans; PMC7890119 on entrainment frequency of cardiolocomotor synchronization; Royal Society rsfs.2021.0088 on principles of entrainment in biological oscillators.

### How many independent modes actually exist?

**Three.** The locomotor CPG, the cardiac pacemaker, and the respiratory center. And even these three tend toward entrainment at integer ratios during sustained walking, reducing their effective independence.

Muscle synergy research using EMG during walking identifies 4-5 motor modules via NMF/PCA, but these represent activation patterns within the locomotor system, not independent oscillators. The CPG literature models human bipedal locomotion with 2 coupled oscillators (one per leg) forming a single unit-level rhythm generator. PCA of gait kinematics captures 85-95% of variance in 3-5 principal components.

The claim of 7 independent modes is inflated by a factor of roughly 2.3. Three independent generators produce many coupled observable oscillations. A pendulum with 7 sensors attached does not have 7 modes.

## Fractal Dimension of Gait

### Actual measured values from Hausdorff et al.

The Hausdorff et al. 1995 paper ("Is walking a random walk?") measured stride intervals of 10 healthy young men walking at their usual rate for 9 minutes. Using DFA and power spectral analysis, they demonstrated that stride-to-stride fluctuations exhibit long-range correlations extending over hundreds of steps, decaying in a scale-free power-law fashion.

| Study | Population | DFA alpha | Fractal dimension D = 2 - alpha |
|-------|-----------|-----------|--------------------------------|
| Hausdorff et al. 1995 | Healthy young men (n=10) | ~0.76 | ~1.24 |
| Hausdorff et al. 1996 | Healthy young adults | ~0.76 | ~1.24 |
| Meta-analysis (young adults) | Healthy young adults | 0.87 +/- 0.15 | ~1.13 |
| Meta-analysis (elderly) | Healthy elderly | 0.68 +/- 0.14 | ~1.32 |
| Huntington's disease | Pathological | approaches 0.5 | approaches 1.5 |
| Severe pathology | Pathological | ~0.5 | ~1.5 |

The stride interval coefficient of variation is typically 2-4% in healthy adults. Long-range correlations are STABLE across multiple walking sessions and time scales (Hausdorff 1996). Aging reduces alpha (elderly: 0.68 vs young: 0.87). The threshold discriminating young from old: alpha = 0.86.

### DFA exponents and what they mean

For self-affine time series like stride intervals (modeled as fractional Gaussian noise):

    DFA alpha = H (Hurst exponent)
    Fractal dimension D = 2 - H

Therefore:
- alpha = 0.5 --> D = 1.5 --> white noise (uncorrelated, memoryless) --> PATHOLOGICAL
- alpha = 0.76 --> D = 1.24 --> 1/f-like scaling (long-range correlated) --> HEALTHY YOUNG
- alpha = 0.87 --> D = 1.13 --> strong long-range correlations --> HEALTHY YOUNG (meta-analysis)
- alpha = 1.0 --> D = 1.0 --> 1/f noise (maximally correlated) --> theoretical ideal

The key insight from Hausdorff: healthy gait fluctuations are neither periodic (D = 1.0) nor random (D = 1.5). They exhibit fractal scaling in between, indicating a complex, multi-scale control system. This 1/f-like behavior is a signature of healthy physiological complexity (Goldberger's framework).

### Connection to the d ~ 3/2 hypothesis from Round 4

Round 4 established that D = 1.5 corresponds to white noise (alpha = 0.5), not healthy function, through the fGn correction. Round 5 now provides the direct empirical confirmation: the actual measured fractal dimension of healthy gait variability is D ~ 1.13-1.25, not 1.5.

The ESQUISSE's claim that the superposition of 7 oscillatory modes produces d ~ 1.5 is wrong on both ends: there are not 7 independent modes (there are 3), and the healthy fractal dimension is not 1.5 (it is 1.13-1.25). D = 1.5 is the signature of the pathological breakdown of long-range correlations -- precisely what Hausdorff's entire body of work demonstrated healthy gait is NOT.

Claiming d ~ 1.5 for healthy gait inverts the finding. It is like claiming that a healthy heartbeat is arrhythmic.

**Key sources:** Hausdorff et al. 1995, J Appl Physiol 78:349-358; Hausdorff et al. 1996, J Appl Physiol 80:1448-1457; Hausdorff 2007 review (PMC2267927); PMC7174763 -- Fractal Analysis of Human Gait Variability (2020 review); PMC5705160 -- Fractal analyses reveal independent complexity and predictability of gait.

## Golden Ratio in the Kinetic Chain

### The hypothesis (ESQUISSE Problem 8.4)

The ESQUISSE proposes that intersegmental activation delays in rotational athletics follow a Fibonacci-like cascade, where each segment's delay is approximately phi (1.618) times the previous segment's delay. Three rotational sports were examined: baseball pitching, golf swing, and tennis serve.

### Baseball pitching

The most complete quantitative timing data comes from ASMI and related studies. Peak angular velocity timing as percentage of stride-foot-contact to ball-release (total ~145 ms):

| Segment | Timing (% of SFC-to-BR) | Time (ms) |
|---------|--------------------------|-----------|
| Pelvis peak | ~32% | 46 ms |
| Trunk peak | ~52% | 75 ms |
| Shoulder MER | ~81% | 118 ms |
| Elbow extension | ~93% | 134 ms |

Intersegmental delays: 28 ms (pelvis-to-trunk), 43 ms (trunk-to-shoulder), 17 ms (shoulder-to-elbow).

**Successive delay ratios: 0.661 and 2.565.** Neither is close to phi (1.618).

The first ratio (0.66) is the INVERSE of what a Fibonacci cascade would predict -- the pelvis-to-trunk delay is SHORTER than the trunk-to-shoulder delay. The pattern is short-long-short, not the monotonically decreasing cascade the hypothesis requires.

Youth pitcher data shows even worse agreement: delay ratios of 0.280 and 1.908.

Peak shoulder internal rotation velocity reaches ~7,000-7,500 deg/s, occurring 3-4 ms AFTER ball release. The entire acceleration phase (MER to ball release) lasts only 42-58 ms.

**Key sources:** PMC3445080 (Kinetic Chain in Overhand Pitching review); IJSPT article 75224 (youth pitchers); Fleisig et al. 1999 (J Biomechanics); PMC7919348 (kinematic sequence patterns).

### Golf swing

The PGA Tour average downswing duration is approximately 248 +/- 4 ms. The TPI kinematic sequence research describes the delay pattern as:

- LARGE gap between pelvis and thorax peaks (~35 ms)
- VERY SMALL gap between thorax and arm peaks (~20 ms)
- LARGE gap between arm and club peaks (~35 ms)

This is a U-shaped pattern (large-small-large), which is fundamentally incompatible with a Fibonacci cascade (which requires monotonically decreasing delays).

**Estimated delay ratios: 1.750 and 0.571.** The second ratio falls below 1.0, meaning delays INCREASE at the distal end.

Additionally, the thorax reaches its absolute peak angular velocity AFTER impact in the follow-through (~929 deg/s), not during the downswing (~552 deg/s). The simple four-segment cascade model does not accurately represent the golf swing.

**Key sources:** TPI Kinematic Sequence research; PMC6246863 (Golf Swing Rotational Velocity); Cheetham et al. 2008; PMC3599250 (Upper torso and pelvis linear velocity).

### Tennis serve

A 2024 study of Dutch professional tennis players using 1000 Hz IMU systems found that the tennis serve VIOLATES proximal-to-distal sequencing entirely:

| Transition | First serve delay | Second serve delay |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------|
| Pelvis-to-trunk | -28.3 ms (SD 33.5) | -28.9 ms (SD 27.0) |
| Trunk-to-upper-arm | 124.5 ms (SD 14.3) | 127.3 ms (SD 13.4) |

The trunk peaks approximately 28 ms BEFORE the pelvis. The first delay is negative. No meaningful ratio can be computed. The pattern is categorically different from any cascade.

The study explicitly found that intersegmental timing was NOT significantly associated with ball speed. Only segment angular velocity magnitudes mattered.

Peak angular velocities (first serve): pelvis 586.8 deg/s, trunk 897.2 deg/s, upper arm 3,206.6 deg/s.

**Key sources:** PMC11746891 (Frontiers in Sports and Active Living, 2024); Martin et al. 2014; PMC2577481.

### Summary of computed ratios across all sports

| Sport | Delay ratios | Distance from phi (1.618) |
|-------|-------------|---------------------------|
| Baseball (professional) | 0.661, 2.565 | -59%, +59% |
| Baseball (youth) | 0.280, 1.908 | -83%, +18% |
| Golf (estimated) | 1.750, 0.571 | +8%, -65% |
| Tennis serve | negative/undefined | N/A |

For comparison, the gait stance/swing ratio = 1.629 +/- 0.173 is genuinely close to phi. The kinetic chain timing ratios are not.

### What the published literature actually shows about phi in biomechanics

The golden ratio genuinely appears in CYCLIC locomotor movements:

- **Gait phase proportions:** Stance/swing duration ratio = 1.629 +/- 0.173, not significantly different from phi (P=0.820). Foot-off occurs at 60-62% of the gait cycle. The golden ratio disappears in neurological pathologies (Iosa et al. 2013, PMC3687768).
- **Swimming stroke phases:** Fibonacci-like phase duration proportions in elite swimmers.
- **Tennis forehand phase durations:** Near-perfect phi ratios in elite players' stroke phase subdivisions (Phi-bonacci index as low as 0.19% deviation). From Frontiers in Human Neuroscience 2025 (PMC12011819).

But these findings apply to PHASE DURATIONS within a cyclic movement -- temporal subdivisions of a single movement's structure. They are mechanistically unrelated to INTERSEGMENTAL DELAYS in ballistic kinetic chains. The 2025 paper's own data shows deviations increasing dramatically at finer temporal subdivisions (from 0% to 50%), suggesting the phi relationship is approximate at coarse scales and breaks down at kinetic-chain-timing resolution.

### What classical biomechanics says about optimal segment timing

The summation-of-speed principle (Bunn, 1972) states: each distal segment should begin its acceleration at or near the moment of peak velocity of the proximal segment. The principle specifies a TRIGGERING CONDITION, not a fixed ratio. The actual delays are determined by:

1. Moment of inertia of each segment (trunk/upper-arm MOI ratio: ~80; arm/forearm: ~3.6; forearm/hand: ~7.0 -- spanning two orders of magnitude)
2. Available muscle torque at each joint
3. Stretch-shortening cycle dynamics
4. Positional requirements at release/impact

None of 208 analyzed baseball pitches showed a perfectly proximal-to-distal sequence (PMC7919348). The most common sequence was pelvis-trunk-arm-hand-forearm (forearm AFTER hand). In tennis, timing was less important than angular velocity magnitudes. The timing problem is fundamentally task-specific and individually variable, not governed by a universal mathematical constant.

## The Superposition Equation: Salvageable?

### Even with 3 modes, is the superposition framing useful?

The superposition equation in the ESQUISSE is:

    u(t) = SUM_{i=1}^{n} A_i sin(2*pi*f_i*t + phi_i) + epsilon(t)

With n reduced from 7 to 3 (locomotor, cardiac, respiratory), the equation is still physically meaningful -- there ARE three concurrent rhythms during walking. But the theoretical payload changes fundamentally:

**What the equation can describe:** Three coupled oscillators producing a quasi-periodic trajectory whose character depends on the coupling strength and frequency ratios. This is a well-studied problem in nonlinear dynamics.

**What the equation cannot do:** Predict a fractal dimension of 1.5 from incommensurate frequency ratios. With only 3 modes that actively entrain to integer ratios (2:1 locomotor-respiratory is dominant), the trajectory during steady-state walking is closer to periodic than quasi-periodic. The fractal character of gait variability (alpha ~ 0.76-0.87) arises from the FLUCTUATIONS of these oscillators -- stride-to-stride variability in timing, not from the superposition of fixed-frequency sinusoids.

The superposition equation describes the DETERMINISTIC skeleton of gait. The fractal dimension arises from the STOCHASTIC fluctuations around that skeleton. These are different mathematical objects. The former is a Fourier sum; the latter is a fractional noise process. Conflating them is like conflating a building's blueprint with its weathering pattern.

### How does coupling vs independence affect the fractal dimension prediction?

The ESQUISSE's implicit argument: 7 incommensurate frequencies --> quasi-periodic trajectory --> fractal dimension d ~ 1.5.

The actual situation: 3 frequencies that entrain to integer ratios --> near-periodic trajectory --> fractal dimension of the deterministic trajectory approaches 1.0 (a smooth curve). The observed D ~ 1.13-1.25 comes from the fractal character of the NOISE (stride-to-stride variability), not from the superposition of independent oscillators.

This means the superposition framing, while physically real, does not do the mathematical work the ESQUISSE needs it to do. The fractal signature of healthy movement is a property of the control system's noise structure, not of the frequency architecture of the oscillators.

### The right framing

A more accurate equation would be:

    tau_n = mu + sigma * xi(n)

where tau_n is the nth stride interval, mu is the mean stride interval, sigma is the variability amplitude, and xi(n) is a fractional Gaussian noise process with Hurst exponent H ~ 0.76-0.87. The fractal dimension D = 2 - H ~ 1.13-1.24 emerges from the long-range correlations in xi(n), not from any superposition of oscillatory modes.

## Implications for the Framework

### What falls

1. **The 7-mode architecture (DEF-08) as physically grounded in locomotor biomechanics.** There are 3 independent oscillatory generators, not 7. The number 7 may still function as a design parameter for the PDE lattice (Round 3 validated 7 Orders on information-theoretic grounds), but it cannot be justified by counting oscillatory modes during walking.

2. **The incommensurability argument (Problem 8.2).** Biological oscillators entrain to integer ratios. The quasi-periodic trajectory that was supposed to produce d ~ 1.5 does not exist during steady-state walking. Entrainment is a feature of healthy movement, not a failure of it.

3. **The golden ratio kinetic chain hypothesis (Problem 8.4).** No published data supports phi appearing in intersegmental activation delays. Across three sports, computed ratios range from 0.28 to 2.57 with no convergence toward 1.618. The tennis serve violates the basic prerequisite of proximal-to-distal ordering.

4. **d ~ 1.5 as the fractal dimension of healthy movement.** Confirmed REFUTED with direct empirical data. Healthy gait: D ~ 1.13-1.25. D = 1.5 = white noise = pathological. This strengthens Round 4's theoretical correction with primary experimental evidence.

### What survives

1. **The general principle that human movement involves superimposed oscillatory systems.** True, but fewer than claimed, and coupled rather than independent.

2. **Phi in cyclic movement phase proportions.** The golden ratio genuinely appears in gait stance/swing ratios (1.629 ~ phi) and other cyclic phase subdivisions. This is a real phenomenon with published support, but it applies to temporal self-similarity within a cycle, not to kinetic chain timing.

3. **The lattice architecture (7 Orders, 4,032 addresses).** Round 3's information-theoretic justification for the lattice remains unaffected. The 7-Order choice is validated by coprimality properties and encoding efficiency, independent of oscillatory mode counts.

4. **The 73% entropy rule from Round 4.** The critical entropy fraction H*/H_max = 0.73 is unaffected by these findings and remains the strongest quantitative result of the research program.

### What transforms

The ESQUISSE's superposition equation should be reconceived. Instead of:

*"7 incommensurate oscillators produce a quasi-periodic fractal trajectory at d ~ 1.5"*

The evidence supports:

*"3 coupled oscillators produce a near-periodic locomotor skeleton. The fractal character of healthy movement (D ~ 1.13-1.25) arises from long-range correlated fluctuations in the control system's timing variability, governed by DFA alpha ~ 0.76-0.87. The golden ratio appears not in kinetic chain timing but in the phase proportions of cyclic movements (stance/swing ~ phi), suggesting a temporal self-similarity principle in locomotor organization."*
